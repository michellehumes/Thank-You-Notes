## Finance Management Overview

### Why Tracking Your Finances Is Non-Negotiable

Here is the truth that separates freelancers who build wealth from those who just stay busy: **if you do not know your numbers, you do not have a business.** You have an expensive hobby.

Most freelancers avoid their finances until tax season, then scramble to piece together a year's worth of transactions from bank statements and memory. That approach costs you money in missed deductions, late fees, and poor pricing decisions. It also costs you sleep.

This module gives you every tool you need to take full command of your money, from the invoice you send to the tax return you file.

---

### The 3 Pillars of Freelance Finance

**Everything in this module is built on three pillars. Master all three and you will always know exactly where your business stands financially.**

---

#### PILLAR 1: Income Tracking

You need to know exactly how much money is coming in, from whom, and when. This is not just about depositing checks. It is about understanding your revenue patterns so you can forecast, plan, and make smart decisions.

**What income tracking tells you:**
- Which clients generate the most revenue
- Which months are strongest and weakest
- Whether you are actually getting paid on time
- How your revenue compares to your goals

---

#### PILLAR 2: Expense Tracking

Every dollar that leaves your business needs to be categorized and recorded. Not just for taxes, but so you can see whether your spending is aligned with your growth.

**What expense tracking tells you:**
- Your true cost of doing business
- Which expenses are eating into your profit
- What you can deduct at tax time
- Where you can cut back or invest more

---

#### PILLAR 3: Tax Preparation

As a freelancer, no one is withholding taxes from your income. That means you are responsible for estimating, saving, and paying your own taxes, including self-employment tax. The tools in this module help you stay ahead of your tax obligations year-round so there are no surprises in April.

**What proactive tax prep gives you:**
- No panic at tax time
- No underpayment penalties
- Maximum deductions captured
- Clear records if you are ever audited

## Payment Follow-Up Email Templates

Late payments are not personal, but they are your problem to solve. Having pre-written follow-up emails removes the emotional friction from chasing money. Send these at the exact intervals noted. Be professional, be direct, and never apologize for expecting to be paid for your work.

---

### STAGE 1: Friendly Reminder (On Due Date)

**Send this on the day payment is due if you have not received it.**

---

**Subject:** Invoice [#___] - Payment Due Today

Hi [Client Name],

I hope you are doing well. I am writing to let you know that Invoice [#___] for $[___] is due today, [Date].

For your convenience, here is a summary:

- **Invoice Number:** [___]
- **Amount Due:** $[___]
- **Due Date:** [Date]
- **Project:** [Brief Description]

You can submit payment via [preferred payment method]. I have reattached the invoice for your reference.

Please let me know if you have any questions or if payment has already been sent.

Thank you,
[Your Name]
[Your Business Name]
[Your Email]
[Your Phone]

---

### STAGE 2: Firm Follow-Up (7 Days Past Due)

**Send this exactly 7 days after the due date if payment has not been received.**

---

**Subject:** Invoice [#___] - 7 Days Past Due

Hi [Client Name],

I am following up regarding Invoice [#___] for $[___], which was due on [Original Due Date]. As of today, payment has not been received.

- **Invoice Number:** [___]
- **Amount Due:** $[___]
- **Original Due Date:** [Date]
- **Days Overdue:** 7

I understand that things can get busy, and I want to make sure this does not slip through the cracks. Could you please confirm when I can expect payment?

If there is an issue with the invoice or the work delivered, I am happy to discuss it. Otherwise, I would appreciate payment at your earliest convenience.

Per our agreement, a late fee of [___]% may apply to overdue balances.

Thank you for your prompt attention.

[Your Name]
[Your Business Name]
[Your Email]
[Your Phone]

---

### STAGE 3: Final Notice (30 Days Past Due)

**Send this at 30 days past due. This is your last email before escalating.**

---

**Subject:** FINAL NOTICE - Invoice [#___] - 30 Days Past Due

Dear [Client Name],

This is a final notice regarding Invoice [#___] for $[___], originally due on [Original Due Date]. Despite previous reminders sent on [dates of prior emails], payment remains outstanding.

**Outstanding Balance:**

| | |
|---|---|
| Original Amount: | $[___] |
| Late Fee ([___]%): | $[___] |
| **Total Amount Due:** | **$[___]** |

I value our working relationship and would like to resolve this matter promptly. Please submit payment within **7 business days** of this notice.

If payment is not received by [Final Deadline Date], I will need to:

- Pause all current and future work
- Pursue additional collection measures as outlined in our contract
- Report the outstanding balance as necessary

If there are circumstances preventing payment, please contact me immediately so we can discuss a resolution.

Respectfully,
[Your Name]
[Your Business Name]
[Your Email]
[Your Phone]

---

> **Follow-Up Tip:** After sending Stage 3, wait the 7 business days. If there is still no response, consider sending a formal demand letter, hiring a collections agency, or filing in small claims court depending on the amount. Document every communication.

## Business vs. Personal Expense Guide

One of the most common mistakes freelancers make is either claiming personal expenses as business deductions (which can trigger an audit) or failing to claim legitimate business expenses (which means overpaying taxes). Use this guide as a quick reference for what qualifies and what does not.

> **Disclaimer:** This guide provides general information only and is not tax advice. Rules can vary based on your specific situation. Always consult a qualified tax professional for definitive guidance.

---

### The Golden Rule

**An expense is a business deduction if it is both ordinary (common in your industry) and necessary (helpful and appropriate for your business).** If an expense is purely personal, it is never deductible. If it is mixed-use, only the business percentage is deductible.

---

### Quick Reference Guide

#### CLEARLY BUSINESS (Generally Deductible)

| Expense | Why It Qualifies |
|---|---|
| Client project supplies | Directly used to deliver client work |
| Business-only software | Used exclusively for business operations |
| Professional liability insurance | Protects your business |
| Business cards and marketing | Promotes your business |
| Client meals (50%) | Business development and relationship building |
| Industry conferences | Professional development in your field |
| Dedicated home office | Space used exclusively for business |
| Business phone line | Used exclusively for business communication |
| Accounting and legal fees | Professional services for your business |
| Website hosting and domain | Your business online presence |
| Contractor and subcontractor payments | Outsourced business work |
| Business bank account fees | Cost of operating your business finances |
| Coworking space fees | Business workspace |

---

#### CLEARLY PERSONAL (Not Deductible)

| Expense | Why It Does Not Qualify |
|---|---|
| Groceries and household items | Personal living expense |
| Personal clothing (even if worn to meetings) | Not specialized to your business |
| Gym membership | Personal health (rare exceptions exist) |
| Personal cell phone (entire bill) | Only business-use percentage qualifies |
| Commute to coworking space | Commuting is personal (but see mileage rules) |
| Childcare | Personal expense (separate tax credits may apply) |
| Personal entertainment | Not a business activity |
| Home internet (entire bill) | Only business-use percentage qualifies |
| Pet expenses | Personal (unless your pet is genuinely part of your business) |
| Vacation travel | Personal leisure |

---

#### MIXED-USE (Partially Deductible - Business Percentage Only)

| Expense | How to Calculate Business Portion |
|---|---|
| **Cell phone** | Track business vs. personal use percentage. If 60% business, deduct 60% of the bill. |
| **Internet** | Calculate based on home office percentage or estimated business use. |
| **Vehicle** | Use mileage log to separate business miles from personal miles. |
| **Home office** | Square footage of office divided by total home square footage. |
| **Computer / Tablet** | If used for both personal and business, estimate business-use percentage. |
| **Travel with personal days** | Only business-related days, meals, and transportation are deductible. |
| **Meals** | Business meals with clients or during business travel: 50% deductible. Personal meals: 0%. |
| **Education** | Deductible if it maintains or improves skills for your current business. Not deductible if it qualifies you for a new career. |

---

### How to Handle Mixed-Use Expenses

**Step 1:** Determine total annual cost of the expense.

**Step 2:** Estimate or calculate the business-use percentage.

**Step 3:** Multiply total cost by business percentage.

**Step 4:** Deduct only the business portion.

- [ ] Claiming meals and entertainment without documentation
- [ ] Rounding all expenses to even numbers (looks estimated, not tracked)
- [ ] Missing or incomplete receipts for large expenses
- [ ] Inconsistent income reporting between your records and 1099s

---

> **When In Doubt:** If you are unsure whether an expense qualifies, ask yourself: "Would I have this expense if I did not have this business?" If the answer is no, it is likely deductible. If yes, it is likely personal. When genuinely uncertain, consult your accountant.

### Key Financial Rules to Remember

1. **Invoice immediately.** The moment work is delivered and approved, the invoice goes out. Every day you delay is a day you delay getting paid.

2. **Track daily, review weekly.** Small consistent habits beat frantic monthly catch-ups.

3. **Pay yourself first, then taxes.** Transfer your tax set-aside before spending a dollar of your revenue.

4. **Your profit margin is your report card.** Revenue means nothing if expenses eat it all. Aim for 50-70% profit margin.

5. **Never chase money silently.** Use the follow-up templates. Be professional, be direct, be persistent.

6. **Receipts are proof.** No receipt means no deduction if you are audited. Capture everything.

7. **Know your daily number.** When you know your daily revenue target, you make better decisions about how you spend your time.
